01
Largest moon

Titan

Bigger than the planet Mercury and wrapped in thick orange haze, Titan has rivers, lakes and seas of liquid methane — the only other world with stable surface liquid.

02
Ocean world

Enceladus

A tiny, brilliant-white moon that fires geysers of water vapour from a hidden global ocean into space — feeding Saturn's faint E ring and hinting at habitability.

03
The Death Star moon

Mimas

Dominated by the enormous Herschel crater, this icy moon bears an uncanny resemblance to a certain fictional battle station.

04
Two-faced moon

Iapetus

One hemisphere shines bright as fresh snow while the other is dark as coal — one of the strangest, most lopsided surfaces in the Solar System.

The Rings

Ice, dust &
starlight

Saturn's rings span up to about 282,000 km — roughly the Earth–Moon distance — yet in most places they are only around 10 metres thick. Built from 99.8% water ice, the particles range from specks of dust to chunks the size of houses, herded into crisp gaps by tiny shepherd moons. Remarkably, they may be only ~100 million years old, meaning the dinosaurs never saw them.
